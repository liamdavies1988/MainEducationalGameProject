"After submitting my project outline, I decided to map out exactly how the game would function before writing any code. I didn’t want to just guess how things should work; I wanted a solid plan that addressed the three main coding challenges I identified: the Core Game, the Coin System, and the Farm Mini-game.
I started by looking at how my own kids used educational games and realized that a simple, clear layout is what works best for 5 to 7-year-olds. I created a flowchart to map out the 'learning loop.' I decided on a 10-question system where the child isn't punished for wrong answers; instead, they see a 'sad face' on their avatar and get to try again until they succeed. This keeps the experience positive and focused on learning.
Next, I built a UML class diagram to organize the technical side. I realized I needed a 'GameManager' to act as the brain of the project, handling the coins and the questions, and a 'PlayerData' script to make sure the student's name, coins, and farm animals are all saved properly. For the farm itself, I planned out an 'Animal' script that handles the walking AI and the sound effects when clicked, making the reward for learning feel more interactive.
By getting these diagrams done early, I’ve moved from a broad idea to a specific technical roadmap. This ensures that when I start building in Unity, the 'Farm' mini-game and the 'Question' engine will talk to each other perfectly through the coin system."
